MIM is a development of the traditional PM process. The standard PM process is to compact a lubricated powder mix in a rigid die by uniaxial pressure, eject the compact from the die, and sinter it. Quite complicated shapes can be produced by the million, but because the part must be ejected, i.e. pushed out of the die cavity then parts with undercuts or projections at right angles to the pressing direction cannot be made directly. This limitation is substantially removed by the MIM process.

The use of the injection moulding of thermo-plastic for the production of quite intricate parts has been common for many years, and such parts are relatively cheap. However, they have poor mechanical properties for engineering applications. MIM incorporates a very high volume fraction of metal powder in a mix to produce a plastic-bonded metal or ceramic part. The careful removal of the plastic binder leaves a skeleton of metal or ceramic which, that can be sintered in much the same way as die compacted parts.

After sintering densities of 95% or more are reached and the mechanical properties are, generally, superior to those of traditional PM parts.

If the shape allows the production of the part by conventional pressing and sintering then MIM would in most cases be too expensive.

However, if the required number of complex shaped parts is higher than a certain amount MIM is cheaper than machining.

The effect of the volume production on cost is that, for example, for a small part weighing 4.5g the cost per part falls from $1.4 for an annual production of 250,000 pieces to $0.2 for 3 million or more (although the bigger the part the smaller is the gap between the cost of 250,000 and 3 million pieces).

A competing process to MIM is investment casting, and for most characteristics of parts produced by the two processes MIM is superior, especially in the case of high part numbers of castings, and of course in non-castable alloys, and many shapes that are possible by MIM cannot be produced by other routes.
