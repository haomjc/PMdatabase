Material Designation Codes
CONVENTIONAL PM MATERIAL DESIGNATIONS :

In this database, where applicable, materials are referred to by codes based on those used in ISO 5755:2001 and MPIF Standard 35 for P/M Structural Parts. Only the sections of the standard codes that relate specifically to chemical composition are used in this database; the suffixes that relate to minimum strength level and heat treatment condition are omitted.

The bases of the standard codes are described as follows:

ISO 5755:2001 Material Designation Codes

(1) Structure of material designation codes

The codes based on the ISO 5755 system comprise two groups as follows:

First group

The first group consists of one to three capital letters, describing the base metal and ways of adding alloying elements:

F = Plain iron powder or iron powder with admixed alloying additives
FD = Iron powder with diffusion-alloyed additives
FL = Pre-alloyed steel powder
FX = Copper-infiltrated steel
C = Copper powder with admixed alloying additives
CL = Pre-alloyed copper-base powder
FLD = Pre-alloyed steel powder with diffusion-alloyed additives (this will be used in future revisions)
FLA = Pre-alloyed steel powder with admixed alloying additives (this will be used in future revisions)

Second group

The second group consists of two to six alpha-numeric characters with two digits for the content of dissolved (combined) carbon without a decimal point (except for copper-based materials and stainless steels) e.g. 03 = 0.3%C. The third position in this group will be the capital letter assigned to the alloying element with the highest content (if any) followed by the content with one or two digits, e.g. 05 = 0.5%, 10 = 10%, 2 = 2.0% of the element in question. Finally this group ends with the capital letter assigned to the element with the second highest content (if any) but without any indication of the content.

The groups within the material code are separated by a hyphen.

(2) Letters assigned to alloying elements

Letters assigned to alloying elements are as follows: C = Copper; G = Graphite; M = Molybdenum, N = Nickel; P = Phosphorus; T = Tin; Z = Zinc.

(3) Examples of the designation system

EXAMPLE 1: C-T10 is a copper-based alloy with 10% added tin.

EXAMPLE 2: F-08C2 is an iron-based material with 0.8% carbon, 2% copper.

EXAMPLE 3: FD-05N4C is an iron-based alloy with 0.5% carbon and diffusion-alloyed additions of nickel (4%) and copper.

EXAMPLE 4: FL-05N2M is a pre-alloyed nickel (2%) and molybdenum steel with 0.5% carbon.

EXAMPLE 5: FX-08C20 is a copper infiltrated iron-base material.

EXAMPLE 6: FL-304 is a type 304 stainless steel.

NOTE The materials designated FLA may have elemental additions to the pre-alloyed base powder. In these circumstances the elemental additions will be shown as a third group.

As an example FLA-05M2-N4C2 would be a pre-alloyed nominal 2% molybdenum steel powder with 0,5% carbon, to which has been added 4% nickel and 2% copper in elemental form.

MPIF Standard 35 Material Code Designations

The codes based on the MPIF Standard 35 for structural P/M parts define a specific material as to chemistry. For example, FC-0208 is a P/M copper steel material containing a nominal 2% copper and 0.8% combined carbon.

In the coding system, the prefix letters denote the general type of material. For example, the prefix "CT" represents copper (C) and tin (T) which is known as bronze.

Prefix and Four Digit Code

The four digits following the prefix letter code refer to the composition of the material.

In nonferrous materials, the first two numbers in the four digit series designate the percentage of the major alloying constituent. The last two numbers of the four digit series designate the percentage of the minor alloying constituent.

For improved machinability lead is sometimes the third alloying element in a nonferrous alloy system. Lead will only be indicated by the letter "P" in the prefix. The percentage of lead or any other minor alloying element which happens to be excluded from the four digit nomenclature, is represented in the "Chemical Composition" which appears with each standard material. Illustration of P/M nonferrous material designation coding:



In ferrous materials, the major alloying elements (except combined carbon) are included in the prefix letter code. Other elements are excluded from the code but are represented in the "Chemical Composition" which appears with each Standard material. The first two digits of the four-digit code indicate the percentage of the major alloying constituent present.

Combined carbon content in ferrous materials is designated by the last two numbers in the four-digit series. The carbon content up to and including 0.3% is considered as zero; higher contents are included in ranges and coded as follows:

In the case of P/M stainless steels and P/M prealloyed low alloy steels, the four-digit series is replaced with a designation derived from modifications of the American Iron and Steel Institute alloy coding system, e.g., SS-316L, FL-4605.

When a P/M prealloyed low alloy steel powder is modified with elemental additions, a three or four alpha-numeric designator is used, e.g., FLN-4205.

The ISO committee is still developing MIM grade designations and, to date, none have been formally adopted.

Therefore, where applicable, MIM grades in the database are referred to by the MPIF designation codes.

For MIM grades not covered by MPIF Standard 35, designation codes in the database have a MIM- prefix, followed by either:

  • An equivalent wrought code. For ferrous materials, including stainless steels, these codes have generally been drawn from the AISI/SAE specifications

    • or
  • A descriptor based directly on the material composition
